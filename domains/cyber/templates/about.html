Scoring Methodology

CyberEdge uses two scoring models. CVEs are scored on intrinsic danger. Aggregated entities (products, vendors, weaknesses, attack patterns) are scored on the proportion of their CVEs that show real-world exploitation signals.

CVE Scoring (0-100)

Three dimensions, summed and capped at 100. Each dimension measures a different aspect of the vulnerability's danger.

Severity (0-33)

CVSS base score (scaled), plus bonuses for low attack complexity and network attack vector. Measures how bad it is if exploited.

Exploitability (0-34)

Log-scaled EPSS probability, plus bonuses for CISA KEV listing (+7) and public exploits on Exploit-DB (+7). Measures how likely exploitation is.

Exposure (0-33)

Log-scaled count of affected software products. A CVE affecting 500 products scores higher than one affecting 3. Measures blast radius.

Product, Vendor & Weakness Scoring (0-100)

Two dimensions, summed and capped at 100. Scores are based on the proportion of an entity's CVEs that show exploitation signals — not raw counts. A product with 6 CVEs all actively exploited scores higher than one with 10,000 mostly-benign CVEs.

Active Threat (0-50)

Based on the proportion of CVEs with EPSS > 10% (high probability of exploitation in the next 30 days), adjusted by Wilson score confidence interval.

Exploit Availability (0-50)

Based on the proportion of CVEs with public exploit code (Exploit-DB) or CISA KEV confirmation of active exploitation, adjusted by Wilson score confidence interval.

Why Wilson scores? Raw proportions give unearned certainty to small samples. A product with 2 CVEs both exploited would naively score 100% — the same as one with 600 out of 600. The Wilson score lower bound adjusts for sample size: 2/2 yields ~15% credible rate, while 600/600 yields ~99%. This is the same math Reddit uses for ranking.

Limitations

  • Scores are based on publicly available data. Zero-day vulnerabilities and private exploitation are not captured.
  • EPSS probabilities are estimates, not certainties. A low EPSS score does not mean a CVE is safe.
  • Product matching relies on CPE identifiers, which can be inconsistent across vendors.
  • Wilson confidence adjustments penalise products with few CVEs. A product with 1 critical CVE may score lower than one with 100 moderate CVEs.
